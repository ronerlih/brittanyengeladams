Artist statement1

I’ve been performing for other people my whole life. I find myself wondering how I move?—or how’d I like to move my body? I know how to move for somebody else. I know how to be a virtuosic modern dancer and get the leg-up––or a post-modern dancer, understated and pedestrian like Judson dance theater.

I'm interested in creating new forms of representation for dance that reflect and value the histories and experiences collected in my body. I am re-imagining new ways of being represented on the stage. I feel like I need to refuse2 the ways I’ve been expected to move, think, perform and behave in order to do that.

The first dances I made were with my sister. We would appropriate material from the songs and the dances we observed on television, radio, movies, etc. We would host performances at our home, performing for family members like my mom and grandparents, sometimes even charging a fee for admittance. We created songs like “Dixie-ba-boo” which is about our beloved cat Dixie, using the melody from the “Cruella Deville Song” sourced from the 101 Dalmatians Movie. We would make dance routines or sometimes improvise movements to go along with the songs. Once my sister Anya received a video camera for Hanukkah and so we became movie makers. Reenacting scenes from Jerry Springer, or writing scripts for scary movies or stories about a problem child. Inviting our friends to play the roles of these imagined characters. My sister would usually act as a director to the films, and I would play the most eccentric, wild, off-ball character in the story—these roles seemed fitting. It’s interesting to think about how my current studio practice resembles the ones from my adolescence. My sister and I riffed on the things we were seeing and hearing by repeating or remixing them in new ways. I think what has changed the most about my studio practice from then to now is its ability to be reflective because of the role writing plays in my process. The writings are reflections of the practice. Sometimes they help to map out what happened. But I like to think of it as a way of documenting my activities. Like a journal or diary.

These acts of refusals are a protest against people and institutions that have undervalued my work and what I represent.     12/4/2019 I use the story of an “aggressive” coyote to describe the ecology of New Brunswick NJ. An “aggressive” coyote was spotted roaming the streets of New Brunswick. This coyote had allegedly bitten a couple of people. New Brunswick NJ is surrounded largely by Rutgers University. I teach in the Dance department on the Douglass campus. I did not see the coyote; I’d only been receiving emails from Rutgers administrators and local law enforcement. They were monitoring the activities of the coyote for weeks. The subject of these emails were titled – “Aggressive Coyote.” My reaction was that of concern for the coyote. The animal had clearly found itself in a foreign place. Displaced from its natural habitat. Though these factors did not seem to be considered, especially to those relaying the news. The news carriers seemed to intend on instilling threat and fear – deeming the animal “aggressive” like a criminal. As if there is a normal type of behavior or response for a wild animal to portray or enact—especially in unsuitable environments that are unfit for a coyote. Why didn’t a local wildlife representative intervene?— Why was there no one to care for or consider the well-being of this living creature? I imagined it was lost, frightened and driven out by a shrinking wilderness, with nowhere to go. In many ways, I can relate to the unintelligible creature and the overwhelming effects a toxic ecology has on behavior and feelings of unbelonging. Today, it was confirmed, the fate of the coyote. It did not have rabies to justify erratic behavior. The coyote had been shot by a local New Brunswick police officer. No more “aggressive” coyote. Problem solved? back
