Artist statement1

I’ve been performing for other people my whole life. I find myself wondering how I move?—or how’d I like to move my body? I know how to move for somebody else. I know how to be a virtuosic modern dancer and get the leg-up––or a post-modern dancer, understated and pedestrian like Judson dance theater.

I'm interested in creating new forms of representation for dance that reflect and value the histories and experiences collected in my body. I want to re-imagine new ways of being represented on the stage. I feel like I need to refuse2 the ways I’ve been expected to move, think, perform and behave in order to do that.

The first dances I made were with my sister. We would use material from the songs and dances we saw on tv and movies, or heard on the radio. We would host performances at our home, performing for family members like my mom and grandparents, sometimes even charging a fee3 for admittance. We created songs like “Dixie-Ba-Boo” which is about our beloved cat Dixie, using the melody from the “Cruella Deville Song” sourced from the 101 Dalmatians Movie. The dance routines were choreographed or sometimes improvised movements to go along with a song. On time my sister Anya received a video camera for Hanukkah and we became movie makers. Reenacting4 scenes from the Jerry Springer show, or writing scripts for scary movies, or stories about a problem child. Inviting our friends to play the roles of these imagined characters. My sister would usually act as a director to the films, and I would play the most aberrant and offbeat character in the story—–these roles5 seemed fitting. The difference between then and now is my attempt to document my movements, whether in practice, performance or everyday life. I write reflections to describe these moment, like keeping a journal or a diary6.

  • Diary

    12/4/2019 I use the story of an “aggressive” coyote to describe the ecology of New Brunswick NJ. An “aggressive” coyote was spotted roaming the streets of New Brunswick. This coyote had allegedly bitten a couple of people. New Brunswick NJ is surrounded largely by Rutgers University. I teach in the Dance department on the Douglass campus. I did not see the coyote; I’d only been receiving emails from Rutgers administrators and local law enforcement. They were monitoring the activities of the coyote for weeks. The subject of these emails were titled – “Aggressive Coyote.” My reaction was that of concern for the coyote. The animal had clearly found itself in a foreign place. Displaced from its natural habitat. Though these factors did not seem to be considered, especially to those relaying the news. The news carriers seemed to intend on instilling threat and fear – deeming the animal “aggressive” like a criminal. As if there is a normal type of behavior or response for a wild animal to portray or enact—especially in unsuitable environments that are unfit for a coyote. Why didn’t a local wildlife representative intervene?— Why was there no one to care for or consider the well-being of this living creature? I imagined it was lost, frightened and driven out by a shrinking wilderness, with nowhere to go. In many ways, I can relate to the unintelligible creature and the overwhelming effects a toxic ecology has on behavior and feelings of unbelonging. Today, it was confirmed, the fate of the coyote. It did not have rabies to justify erratic behavior. The coyote had been shot by a local New Brunswick police officer. No more “aggressive” coyote. Problem solved? back
